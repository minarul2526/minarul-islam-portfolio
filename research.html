Research Overview

My research is centered on a practical question: how can language technologies remain reliable when the input text is complex, shifted, or only partially explicit? Modern transformer models perform strongly on many benchmark datasets, but their predictions can become less dependable when sentiment is shaped by contrast, discourse flow, irony, domain-specific wording, or aspect-level opinion.

I study these limitations through the lens of reliable and context-aware sentiment analysis. Rather than treating a text instance as a single flat sequence, my work examines the linguistic and structural evidence that supports a prediction. This includes clause-level contrast, task-related auxiliary information, sentence-level security evidence, graph-based aspect structure, and uncertainty under distribution shift.

The broader goal is to develop NLP methods that are not only accurate, but also more careful in uncertain settings and more faithful to the evidence in the input. This direction connects sentiment analysis, reliable classification, and software security, with emphasis on model behavior in real-world conditions where data rarely follows a clean benchmark setting.

Current and Recent Projects

Reliable NLP under Distribution Shift

ShiftAware-Select

Selective prediction for safer text classification

Text classifiers are often evaluated as if every input must receive an automatic label. In practice, this assumption is risky when deployment data changes. A model trained on one distribution may still produce confident predictions on unfamiliar or noisy inputs, even when those predictions are wrong.

ShiftAware-Select studies this reliability problem by combining calibration, uncertainty estimation, and coverage-controlled selective prediction. The method gives the classifier a way to answer when confidence is supported and to defer uncertain cases for review. This makes the system better aligned with real decision settings where an incorrect automatic answer may be more costly than asking for human judgment.

Context-Aware Sentiment Analysis

CAGLSAv2

Modeling contrastive clause information

Many sentiment errors occur because the final opinion of a sentence is not simply the average of its words. A review may begin positively and end with a negative contrast, or it may use one clause to qualify the meaning of another. These cases are difficult for models that rely mainly on broad sentence representations.

CAGLSAv2 focuses on this clause-level structure. It uses contrastive information to represent how different parts of a sentence interact before the final sentiment decision is made. The work supports a more context-sensitive view of sentiment, especially for text where contrast changes the direction or strength of the expressed opinion.

Irony-Aware Multitask Sentiment Learning

Handling non-literal opinion in social media text

Social media sentiment is often indirect. A post may contain positive words but communicate criticism, frustration, or negative opinion through irony or emotional framing. When a model reads only the surface wording, it can miss the intended sentiment.

This work treats irony and emotion as related sources of supervision rather than separate afterthoughts. By learning sentiment together with these auxiliary tasks, the model receives additional context about how opinion is expressed. The resulting framework is designed for settings where literal word meaning and intended sentiment do not always match.

DualGraph-RoBERTa for Aspect-Based Sentiment Analysis

Linking opinion evidence to the correct aspect

Aspect-based sentiment analysis becomes difficult when a sentence discusses multiple targets. The same sentence may praise one aspect and criticize another, so a single sentence-level label is not enough. The model needs to know which words support the opinion toward each specific target.

DualGraph-RoBERTa combines transformer representations with graph-based structure to preserve these aspect-specific relationships. The goal is to separate overlapping opinions and identify the local evidence that matters for each aspect. This supports more fine-grained sentiment understanding than standard sentence-level classification.

Discourse-Aware Domain Adaptation for Sentiment Analysis

Improving transfer across domains

A sentiment model trained on one domain often becomes less reliable in another. The challenge is not only that the vocabulary changes. Domains also differ in how people describe aspects, qualify opinions, and organize sentiment-bearing spans.

This research direction studies domain adaptation through discourse-aware and span-level evidence. The aim is to preserve the context behind a prediction while helping the model transfer to new domains where opinion expressions and aspect terminology may be different.

NLP for Software Security

Hierarchical Severity Classification with CWE Enrichment

Structured severity prediction for vulnerability reports

Software vulnerability reports contain dense technical descriptions. Important severity evidence may appear in a few sentences, while the rest of the report provides background or contextual information. A flat document representation can obscure this structure and make the prediction harder to interpret.

This work uses hierarchical modeling to retain sentence-level evidence and enriches the representation with CWE-based security knowledge. The approach is designed to make severity classification more evidence-aware, so the prediction is better connected to the technical details that support it.

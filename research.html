Research Experience

My research focuses on a practical problem in natural language processing. A model may perform well on a benchmark, but it can still fail when the text becomes complex. Sentiment can change because of contrast, irony, hidden meaning, aspect-specific opinion, or domain shift.

I study this problem through reliable and context-aware sentiment analysis. My work does not treat a sentence as only a flat sequence of words. It examines the evidence behind a prediction, such as clause structure, discourse flow, auxiliary emotion or irony cues, aspect relations, and uncertainty under shift.

The goal is to build NLP methods that are accurate and also careful. A reliable model should know when the input is uncertain. A context-aware model should use the right evidence before making a prediction. This direction connects sentiment analysis, reliable classification, and software security.

Current and Recent Projects

Reliable NLP under Distribution Shift

ShiftAware-Select

Selective prediction for safer text classification

Text classifiers usually give a label for every input. This is risky in real use. A model trained on one type of data may face unfamiliar or noisy text after deployment. It may still give a confident answer even when the answer is wrong.

ShiftAware-Select addresses this problem through calibration, uncertainty estimation, and selective prediction. The model predicts when confidence is supported. It defers uncertain cases for review. This makes the system safer in settings where a wrong automatic decision can be costly.

Context-Aware Sentiment Analysis

CAGLSAv2

Modeling contrastive clause information

Sentiment is not always the sum of positive and negative words. A review can start with praise and end with criticism. A later clause can also reverse or weaken the meaning of an earlier clause.

CAGLSAv2 focuses on this clause-level structure. It models how different parts of a sentence interact before the final sentiment decision is made. This helps the model handle text where contrast changes the direction or strength of opinion.

Irony-Aware Multitask Sentiment Learning

Handling non-literal opinion in social media text

Social media sentiment is often indirect. A post may use positive words while expressing criticism or frustration. In these cases, literal word meaning does not match the intended sentiment.

This work learns sentiment together with irony and emotion signals. These related tasks provide extra context about how opinion is expressed. The model is designed for short social media text where meaning is often implied rather than stated directly.

Irony Emotion Multitask Learning Social Media Text

DualGraph-RoBERTa for Aspect-Based Sentiment Analysis

Connecting opinion evidence to the correct aspect

Aspect-based sentiment analysis is difficult when one sentence discusses several targets. A sentence may praise one aspect and criticize another. A single sentence-level label cannot capture this difference.

DualGraph-RoBERTa combines transformer representations with graph-based structure. The goal is to preserve the relationship between each aspect and its supporting opinion words. This gives a more fine-grained view of sentiment than standard sentence-level classification.

Discourse-Aware Domain Adaptation for Sentiment Analysis

Improving transfer across domains

A model trained in one domain may not work well in another. The challenge is not only vocabulary change. Domains also differ in how people describe aspects and organize opinion.

This research studies domain transfer using discourse-aware and span-level evidence. The aim is to keep the context behind a prediction while helping the model adapt to new domains.

NLP for Software Security

Hierarchical Severity Classification with CWE Enrichment

Evidence-aware severity prediction for vulnerability reports

Vulnerability reports contain dense technical information. Important severity evidence may appear in only a few sentences. A flat document representation can hide these sentences and make the model harder to interpret.

This work uses hierarchical modeling to keep sentence-level evidence visible. It also enriches the representation with CWE-based security knowledge. The goal is to make severity classification more connected to the technical details that support the prediction.
